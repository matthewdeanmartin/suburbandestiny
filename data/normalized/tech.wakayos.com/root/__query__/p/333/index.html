The MED Software Development Life Cycle

Posted on March 12, 2008 by matt

The federal government requires it’s agencies to follow a sofware process, according to The Clinger-Cohen Act (Public Law 104-106, search for division E).  MED, which Managed Evolutionary Development is the SDLC process used by various US government agencies and almost no one else.

  • Department of Justice (DOJ) – Patent And Trademark Office 1993 (reference but no text)
  • National Archives and Records Agency (NARA) — no public links, but referenced in online presentation.
  • National Oceanic and Atmospheric Agency (NOAA) — no public links, but referenced in ConOps document (a typical MED artifact)
  • National Instute for Heath (NIH)
  • Bureau of Land Management (BLM)

The methodology appears to have been born around 2002, and shows up in “Managing Software Deliverables” (c) 2004 by John Rittinghouse.

The methodology is a lightly reformed waterfall process.  The NIH version is representative of the MED directives and I’ll direct all my comments toward that one.

    MED was “invented” at the United States Patent and Trademark Office around 1900. The first MED guidebook was published in 1992 and a revised edition in June 1993. It was based on the principles used in the management of the development of the large scale Patent Search system and was instituted to provide a framework for its extension over time.

    I was the principal developer of the methodology. The Mitre Corporation, which was providing systems support to the USPTO at the time, undertook to collect the various elements of the methodology and to formalize its procedures.
